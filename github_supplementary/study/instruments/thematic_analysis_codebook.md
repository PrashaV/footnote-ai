# Thematic Analysis Codebook
**Footnote User Study — Qualitative Interview Data**  
Method: Reflexive Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006)  
Inter-rater reliability: Cohen's κ = 0.81 (substantial agreement)  
Coders: Two independent researchers; disagreements resolved by discussion

---

## Codebook Development Process

1. **Familiarisation**: Both coders independently read all 10 transcripts in full before coding.
2. **Initial coding**: Open, line-by-line coding applied to first 3 transcripts independently. Codes compared and reconciled.
3. **Theme generation**: Codes grouped into candidate themes; theme names and definitions drafted collaboratively.
4. **Theme review**: All 10 transcripts re-coded against candidate themes. Themes refined where data did not fit.
5. **Final codebook**: Six themes with ≥ 3 participant endorsements retained for reporting.
6. **Reliability check**: 20% of transcript segments (randomly selected) double-coded by both coders. κ = 0.81.

**Unit of analysis**: Utterance (one or more sentences expressing a coherent idea).  
**Endorsement threshold**: A theme is counted for a participant if ≥ 1 utterance is coded to that theme.

## Theme 1: Knowledge Graph as "Map of the Field"
**Endorsements: 9/10 participants**

### Definition
Participant describes the citation knowledge graph as providing spatial, visual orientation in an unfamiliar literature — enabling rapid identification of key papers, authors, or topic clusters without reading individual abstracts.

### Inclusion Criteria
- References the graph as providing overview, orientation, or bird's-eye view
- Mentions ability to see relationships between papers visually
- Describes navigating the graph (zooming, clicking nodes, hovering for abstracts)
- Contrasts the graph's speed with sequential abstract reading
- Uses spatial language: map, landscape, hub, cluster, connected to, centre of

### Exclusion Criteria
- General positive statements about Footnote that do not reference the graph
- References to the knowledge graph that are purely negative/critical
- Statements about citation counts without reference to the visual layout

### Representative Quotes
> "The graph gave me a bird's-eye view in ten seconds that it would take me an hour to reconstruct from scratch by reading abstracts." — P03

> "I could see the citation hubs immediately — the big nodes — those are the papers I actually need to read." — P08

> "It's like a map of the field. I knew where I was within 30 seconds." — P05

> "Clicking on a node and getting the abstract right there — that's so much better than opening 20 tabs." — P01

## Theme 2: Confidence Ratings Reduce Cognitive Load
**Endorsements: 8/10 participants**

### Definition
Participant describes the confidence badges (low/medium/high) on key findings as helping them prioritise which claims require further verification, externalising the epistemic triage process they would otherwise perform mentally.

### Inclusion Criteria
- Direct reference to confidence scores, badges, or levels (low/medium/high)
- Description of using confidence ratings to allocate reading or verification effort
- Statements that ratings told them "what to trust", "where to look harder", or "where to verify"
- Appreciation of the system not presenting all findings as equally certain

### Exclusion Criteria
- Mentions of confidence in the system generally (without reference to the badge feature)
- Statements about hallucination concern unrelated to the confidence badge feature
- Requests for more calibrated confidence without acknowledging the existing feature

### Representative Quotes
> "I could immediately see which claims were well-supported and which were borderline. That told me exactly where I needed to do additional reading." — P05

> "The amber badges made me pause. That's exactly what I wanted — a flag that says look more carefully here." — P09

> "I appreciated that it didn't just tell me things confidently. It admitted when it wasn't sure." — P02

> "The confidence score was the first thing I looked at for every finding. It changed how I read them." — P08

## Theme 3: Literature Review as Scaffolding
**Endorsements: 8/10 participants**

### Definition
Participant frames the AI-generated literature review output as a scaffold or starting structure for their own writing/synthesis, rather than as a finished product. Includes explicit statements that they would not submit the generated text as their own work, but would use it as a foundation.

### Inclusion Criteria
- Uses words like scaffold, structure, framework, starting point, outline, draft
- States they would not submit or directly use AI text verbatim in their own work
- Describes using Footnote output to identify papers to read, as an outline, or as a structural template
- Frames the tool as a "research assistant" rather than a replacement

### Exclusion Criteria
- General statements about AI not replacing researchers (too broad — only code if specific to the literature review output)
- Statements primarily about trust rather than workflow integration
- Statements about speed without reference to the review as a scaffold

### Representative Quotes
> "I wouldn't submit the generated review as my own work, but it gave me the section structure and the key papers to build from." — P09

> "It's like getting a very smart first draft. I still have to do the intellectual work, but I'm not starting from zero." — P01

> "I use it like a very good research assistant who's done an initial scan for me." — P08

> "The literature review panel is basically the skeleton — I fill in the flesh with my own reading." — P03

## Theme 4: Research Gaps as Ideation Aid
**Endorsements: 7/10 participants**

### Definition
Participant describes the research gaps panel as generating novel research directions, questions, or ideas they had not previously considered — particularly valuable for early-career researchers preparing proposals or framing their own work.

### Inclusion Criteria
- Reference to the research gaps panel specifically (not general statements about gaps in a field)
- Statements about discovering new research directions, questions, or ideas
- Description of using gaps to inform grant proposals, thesis topics, or future work plans
- Positive surprise at the specificity or novelty of identified gaps

### Exclusion Criteria
- Complaints that gaps were too broad or obvious (these should be coded to concerns/limitations)
- General statements about the system being comprehensive
- Statements about research gaps that are clearly referring to gaps in retrieved papers (not the panel)

### Representative Quotes
> "It surfaced three gaps I hadn't thought of. I actually bookmarked this query to revisit for my proposal." — P01

> "This is exactly what I needed for my literature review chapter — a clear articulation of what hasn't been done yet." — P04

> "Two of the gaps it identified are essentially the questions I'm working on. That's validating." — P03

> "I showed my supervisor the gaps panel. She said we should use this before every grant meeting." — P02

## Theme 5: Speed as Primary Value Driver
**Endorsements: 10/10 participants (universal)**

### Definition
Participant cites the speed or time savings from Footnote as the primary benefit — and frames this not merely as efficiency but as qualitatively changing the research behaviour they're willing to engage in (e.g., willingness to explore adjacent fields).

### Inclusion Criteria
- Explicit mention of time saved or speed as a benefit
- Statements about reducing the "cost" or "barrier" to exploring new topics
- Comparisons to how long the same task would take without the tool
- Statements about being willing to explore areas they previously avoided due to time cost

### Exclusion Criteria
- Speed mentioned only as a secondary or incidental benefit (when another feature is the primary focus)
- Statements about speed that are negative (e.g., concerns that speed implies shallowness)

### Representative Quotes
> "I now feel comfortable surveying areas adjacent to my speciality that I would previously have avoided because the ramp-up cost was too high." — P10

> "In ten minutes I understood the landscape of a field I had never read in. That would have taken me a week." — P07

> "Even if I don't use everything it produces, just having the summary in 5 minutes is worth it." — P06

> "Speed is the thing. I can now do a literature check as a routine step instead of a major commitment." — P05

> "My Google Scholar task felt like wading through mud compared to this." — P04

## Theme 6: Residual Concern About LLM Hallucination
**Endorsements: 6/10 participants**

### Definition
Participant expresses concern that the system may generate inaccurate or hallucinated content, particularly for niche or specialised sub-topics. This concern may persist even after the participant acknowledges the system's grounding mechanisms (real paper retrieval, confidence scores).

### Inclusion Criteria
- Explicit concern about hallucination, fabrication, or inaccuracy of generated content
- Statements about needing to independently verify claims or paper details
- Concern specific to niche, emerging, or highly specialised domains
- Expressions of distrust toward the synthesis layer even when papers are acknowledged as real

### Exclusion Criteria
- General scepticism about AI that is not specifically about hallucination (too broad)
- Trust concerns about the completeness of retrieved papers (code separately as coverage concern)
- Statements about wanting more papers — these reflect coverage, not hallucination

### Representative Quotes
> "I trust it for broad topics, but for my specific area of gut immunology, I would verify every claim independently." — P06

> "I know the papers it retrieved are real, but the synthesis layer still worries me. I've seen GPT make things up too many times." — P08

> "For my very specialised area, I'm not sure it would find the right papers, and then everything downstream would be wrong." — P07

> "It's not that I distrust it — I just know I can't use it uncritically. I treat it like a smart student who might have made errors." — P09

## Inter-Rater Reliability

**κ = 0.81** (substantial agreement, Landis & Koch, 1977)

Calculated on 20% of transcript utterances (randomly selected, n = 47 utterances from 2 transcripts) double-coded independently by both coders before discussion.

Disagreements arose primarily in:
- Distinguishing Theme 2 (confidence badges) from Theme 6 (general hallucination concern): resolved by requiring Theme 2 to specifically reference the badge/scoring feature
- Distinguishing Theme 3 (scaffolding) from Theme 5 (speed): resolved by requiring Theme 3 to reference the written review output specifically
